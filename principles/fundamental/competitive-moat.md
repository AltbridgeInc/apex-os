# Competitive Moat Analysis

## Principle

A competitive moat is a sustainable competitive advantage that protects a company from competition and allows it to maintain high returns on capital over time. The wider and deeper the moat, the more durable the business.

## Types of Moats

### 1. Network Effects
**Definition**: Product/service becomes more valuable as more people use it

**Examples**:
- Social networks (Facebook, LinkedIn)
- Marketplaces (eBay, Airbnb)
- Payment networks (Visa, Mastercard)

**Indicators**:
- User growth accelerates value
- High switching costs due to network
- Winner-take-most dynamics
- Critical mass already achieved

**Strength**: Very Strong (hardest to disrupt)

### 2. Brand Strength
**Definition**: Brand commands premium pricing and customer loyalty

**Examples**:
- Luxury brands (LVMH, HermÃ¨s)
- Consumer staples (Coca-Cola, Nike)
- Trusted services (Bloomberg, Adobe)

**Indicators**:
- Price premium vs competitors (>20%)
- Customer willingness to pay more
- Brand recognition and trust
- Repeat purchase rates

**Strength**: Strong (but requires maintenance)

### 3. Cost Advantages
**Definition**: Structural cost advantages that competitors cannot match

**Examples**:
- Scale economies (Walmart, Costco)
- Proprietary technology (TSMC)
- Unique assets (pipelines, mines)
- Process advantages (Toyota Production System)

**Indicators**:
- Lower cost per unit than competitors
- Margins maintained even during price wars
- Barriers to achieving similar scale
- Patents or proprietary processes

**Strength**: Strong (if sustainable)

### 4. Switching Costs
**Definition**: High friction or cost for customers to switch to competitor

**Examples**:
- Enterprise software (SAP, Oracle)
- Operating systems (Microsoft, Apple)
- Banking relationships
- Installed equipment (Caterpillar)

**Indicators**:
- Multi-year contracts common
- High integration/training costs
- Data lock-in
- Low churn rates (<5% annually)

**Strength**: Medium-Strong (varies by industry)

### 5. Regulatory/IP Protection
**Definition**: Legal barriers prevent competition

**Examples**:
- Patents (pharmaceuticals)
- Licenses (utilities, casinos)
- Regulatory approvals (medical devices)
- Copyrights (Disney characters)

**Indicators**:
- Patent portfolio strength
- Years of patent protection remaining
- Regulatory barriers to entry
- Licensing requirements

**Strength**: Medium (time-limited, can expire)

## Moat Evaluation

### Score 9-10 (Wide Moat)
- Multiple moat types present
- Moat strengthening over time
- 20%+ ROIC sustained for 10+ years
- Dominant market position (>50% share)
- No credible threat from competitors

**Examples**: Apple (brand + switching + network), Amazon (scale + network), Visa (network effects)

### Score 7-8 (Narrow Moat)
- One strong moat type present
- Moat stable or slowly strengthening
- 15-20% ROIC sustained for 5+ years
- Strong market position (30-50% share)
- Some competitive pressure but manageable

**Examples**: Many quality industrial companies, regional banks with strong relationships

### Score 5-6 (Weak Moat)
- Moat exists but uncertain durability
- ROIC 12-15% but inconsistent
- Moderate market share (15-30%)
- Facing increasing competition
- Technology/regulatory risk

**Examples**: Traditional retailers (pre-Amazon), legacy media companies

### Score 3-4 (No Moat)
- No clear competitive advantage
- ROIC <12% or declining
- Fragmented market (<15% share)
- Easy for competitors to replicate
- Competes primarily on price

**Examples**: Commodity producers, highly competitive industries

### Score 0-2 (Negative Moat)
- Structural disadvantages
- Negative ROIC or margin compression
- Losing market share rapidly
- Disrupted by new technology
- Shrinking TAM

**Examples**: Declining industries (print newspapers), disrupted businesses (Blockbuster)

## Moat Durability Tests

### Test 1: Pricing Power
Can the company raise prices without losing significant customers?
- **Strong Moat**: Regular price increases accepted
- **Weak Moat**: Price increases cause customer defection

### Test 2: Return on Invested Capital
Does the company consistently earn high ROIC?
- **Strong Moat**: ROIC >15% for 10+ years
- **Weak Moat**: ROIC <12% or volatile

### Test 3: Market Share Trends
Is market share stable, growing, or declining?
- **Strong Moat**: Gaining or stable at high level
- **Weak Moat**: Losing share to competitors

### Test 4: Competitive Response
When competitors attack, can the company defend?
- **Strong Moat**: Competitors fail to gain traction
- **Weak Moat**: Loses share in price wars

### Test 5: New Entrant Success
Do new entrants successfully compete?
- **Strong Moat**: New entrants fail or remain niche
- **Weak Moat**: New entrants take significant share

## Red Flags (Moat Erosion)

- **Technology Disruption**: New tech making moat irrelevant
- **Regulatory Changes**: Loss of regulatory protection
- **Customer Power Shift**: Buyers consolidating (reducing switching costs)
- **Substitute Products**: Alternatives emerging
- **ROIC Decline**: Returns on capital compressing
- **Margin Pressure**: Forced to compete on price
- **Market Share Loss**: Losing to competitors consistently

## Application

### Pre-Investment
- Identify primary moat type(s)
- Test moat durability (pricing power, ROIC, share trends)
- Compare to 5-10 years ago (strengthening or weakening?)
- Assess competitive threats
- Assign moat score (0-10)

### During Hold Period
- Monitor ROIC trends quarterly
- Watch for new competitors or technologies
- Track market share changes
- Assess pricing power in earnings calls
- Update moat score

### Exit Triggers
- ROIC declining for 3+ quarters
- Significant market share loss (>5% annually)
- New technology threatening moat
- Regulatory changes removing protection
- Management destroying moat (poor M&A, bad strategy)

## Best Practices

1. **Moat Type Matters**: Network effects and brand are most durable
2. **Multiple Moats Better**: Companies with 2-3 moat types are strongest
3. **Test, Don't Assume**: Just because company claims moat doesn't mean it exists
4. **Watch the Trend**: Moat strengthening > stable > weakening
5. **ROIC is Proof**: High sustained ROIC is evidence of moat

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing size with moat (being big isn't a moat)
- Assuming past moat still exists (moats erode)
- Ignoring technological disruption
- Not comparing to competitors (relative moat matters)
- Overvaluing weak moats (patent protection with 2 years left)
